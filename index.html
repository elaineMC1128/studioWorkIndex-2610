Studio project speculation

Framing

This project explores how music-making can be reimagined as a more intuitive and playful experience, rather than something that requires technical knowledge or formal training. Many traditional music tools rely on structured systems such as timelines, notation, or sequencing, which can make them difficult for beginners to approach. In contrast, contemporary digital media tools increasingly focus on accessibility, allowing users to engage in creative expression through simple and immediate interactions.

For example, tools like Patatap demonstrate how pressing a single key can generate both sound and visual feedback, creating a strong and immediate connection between action and response. This highlights the importance of feedback in helping users understand how an interactive system works. Similarly, the Musical Text Editor reinterprets typing as a musical action, showing that creative input does not need to follow traditional musical formats. Instead, everyday actions can be mapped to expressive outputs.


These examples suggest that creative tools are shifting away from precision and control, and towards play, exploration, and reinterpretation of input. Based on this context, this project speculates that music-making can be approached as a spatial and generative interaction, where users do not directly construct sound, but instead influence a system that produces it.

This idea is also informed by early in-class experiments exploring mapping and feedback. The mapping experiment showed that users quickly understand interactions when there is a consistent relationship between movement and outcome. The feedback experiment highlighted how immediate visual and auditory responses help users perceive cause and effect. Together, these insights suggest that an effective expressive tool can be built through simple rules, as long as the relationship between action and response is clear.

Purpose

The purpose of this project is to design a browser-based interactive tool that allows users to generate evolving sound patterns through spatial interaction. The tool takes the form of a pool-like environment viewed from above, where users can place animated fish into the space.

Rather than acting as direct instruments, the fish behave as autonomous agents. Once placed in the pool, they move independently across a tiled surface. The tile lines function as sound triggers, so that when a fish crosses a line, a musical note is produced. At the same time, the line visually reacts through a small ripple or vibration, reinforcing the connection between movement and sound.


Different fish introduce variation into the system. For instance, larger fish may move more slowly and produce lower tones, while smaller fish move faster and generate higher sounds. By choosing how many fish to place, where to position them, and how they interact within the space, users influence the resulting sound patterns.

This interaction model shifts the focus from direct control to system-based interaction. Instead of composing a fixed sequence of notes, users set up conditions in which sound emerges over time. This approach is influenced by tools such as Blob Opera, where users manipulate behaviour rather than individual notes, and Incredibox, where combining elements leads to layered musical outcomes.

Through this design, the project explores how indirect interaction can still produce meaningful creative expression. Users are not removed from the process, but instead engage with it differently—by observing, adjusting, and experimenting with the system.

Worth

The value of this project lies in its exploration of generative creativity and mediated interaction. By reducing the need for technical knowledge, the tool makes music-making more accessible and encourages users to experiment without fear of making mistakes.

Compared to tools that rely on direct input, this project introduces a layer of separation between the user and the sound. The fish act as intermediaries, meaning that sound is not triggered directly, but emerges through behaviour. This creates a more dynamic and less predictable system, where outcomes are shaped over time rather than immediately defined.

This unpredictability is an important aspect of the experience. It allows users to encounter unexpected patterns and moments of discovery, which can make the interaction more engaging. Instead of focusing on control or precision, the tool encourages exploration and curiosity.

Insights from the design experiments also support this approach. The feedback experiment demonstrated that clear and immediate responses help users understand interactions, which is reflected in the visual and audio responses of the tile lines. The mapping experiment showed that spatial relationships can effectively communicate system behaviour, which is applied in the consistent mapping between fish movement and sound triggers.

More broadly, this project contributes to ongoing discussions in digital media design about how tools can support new forms of creative expression. Rather than treating users as performers who execute actions, it positions them as participants within a system. The creative outcome is not something they directly construct, but something they influence.

Ultimately, this project suggests that interactive tools can move beyond traditional models of control and towards more open, exploratory systems. By combining mapping, feedback, and generative behaviour, it proposes an alternative way of engaging with music—one that is visual, spatial, and shaped through interaction over time.

Conceptual Response

My project is an interactive sequencing tool. It is strongly influenced by experimental music games and aims to free music-making from the complexity of specialist knowledge. Instead of presenting users with a serious and technical music production interface like a traditional DAW, it transforms music composition into a lively and bright “digital pool” landscape, where users create melodies by placing different sea creatures, acting as note carriers, into a gridded pool.

The creative context of this project is centred on making music creation more approachable to a wider audience. Rather than expecting users to understand music theory or learn the conventions of traditional instruments and digital audio workstations, the project explores a more accessible way of composing and performing music. Through a simple, bright, cartoon-like visual style, it introduces digital music-making in a way that feels playful and inviting. The aim is to create an immersive but low-pressure environment for exploring sound, reducing the learning barrier of the interactive tool and allowing people to engage with music in a different way.

The main interaction design principle explored in this project is mapping. In the prototype, the spatial position of each fish is mapped to a specific musical outcome, so moving a fish to a different place in the pool changes the sequence that is produced. In this way, the visual arrangement of the fish becomes a form of musical structure. Rather than simply pressing buttons to trigger fixed notes, the user composes through spatial decisions and placement.

The novelty of the project lies in how it reinterprets the logic of a sequencer through the metaphor of a fish pool. Instead of using the conventional “scan line” found in traditional sequencers, I introduce a mechanism I call Rhythmic Wave Pulses. The sequencing process is no longer represented as a single moving point, but as a wave-like motion with a stronger sense of physical rhythm and spreading movement. In addition, I use a “fan” as a physical control metaphor for adjusting the frequency of the waves, giving users a more unusual and playful controller. This design not only connects closely to the idea of wave pulses, but also moves away from the standard operational language of music tools and turns it into a more intuitive, engaging, and game-like experience. As a result, the relationship between action and outcome becomes more tactile and imaginative, while still remaining clear enough for users to understand and explore.

Technical Approach

This project is a browser-based prototype built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with Konva.js handling the visual interface and Tone.js handling audio playback. HTML and CSS are used to organize the page structure and apply styles, while JavaScript handles the main system logic, including drag-and-drop behavior, playback status, and sequence updates. I chose Konva.js because the project relies on a custom 2D scene that includes layered graphics, draggable objects, hover feedback, and animation effects. Tone.js is used to trigger notes in real-time within the browser and link visual actions to repeatable audio output. This combination allows the project to function as a small interactive sequencer, rather than just a static visual prototype.

The core feature of this project is a drag-and-drop fish sequencer. Users create music by dragging different marine creatures from a tray into a grid-like digital pool, where each placement becomes part of the musical sequence. Visually, the pool serves as both an engaging scene and an easy-to-read grid-based creative space, with the fish acting as note carriers. Functionally, the system detects which grid cell each fish is dragged into and stores the fish’s species and position as sequence data. During playback, sound waves pulse across the tank column by column; when they reach a position occupied by a fish, Tone.js triggers the corresponding sound effect. On the interaction level, the primary operations include dragging, dropping, and testing the arrangement through playback. This allows the creative process to be realized through spatial decisions rather than traditional button inputs. Technically, this functionality is built using Konva’s draggable shapes and layered canvas structure, combined with JavaScript logic for grid detection, object state management, and timed triggers.

Peer User Testing Reflection

Peer user testing indicated that the prototype was generally successful as an engaging music creation tool. Most participants reported that they felt able to create pieces with a personal touch, and many successfully produced music clips they enjoyed.

However, a common issue raised in the feedback was that users did not immediately understand the tool when they first started using it. Several users mentioned that they were initially confused by the fan control feature, particularly not realizing that clicking the fan would start playback or affect the rhythm. Some users were also unsure of the meanings of the different fish when they first entered the interface, and were unclear about the purposes of certain icons and control buttons. This suggests that while the prototype becomes understandable after a brief exploration, its onboarding process and information architecture still require improvement. To address this, I plan to make the controls more intuitive and the interface easier to understand upon first use by enhancing the information architecture, adding brief explanatory labels or prompts, and strengthening the interface’s visual hierarchy.

Another clear trend in the feedback is that users gave positive reviews of the project’s playful visual style and metaphors. Many testers found the fan control design creative, and several described it as “cute,” “fun,” or “in line with their aesthetic expectations.” This is encouraging, as one of my goals was precisely to move away from the visual language of traditional music software and make sequencing more game-like and accessible. At the same time, some users pointed out directions for expanding expressiveness. Suggested improvements include adding more sound types, extending note durations, adding sliders or controls, distinguishing between fish types more clearly, and providing more options for exploring musical effects. This feedback indicates that users feel the creative space and richness currently offered by the tool are somewhat limited, reminding me that I need to expand users’ creative scope and options while maintaining the project’s core focus on ease of use. Other users noted that certain sounds were too harsh, while some bass tones were too quiet. This feedback is also a key focus for adjustments and refinements in the next phase, as it determines the correlation between visual actions and auditory feedback, as well as the overall harmony of the project. I will explore sounds that better suit the tool’s cute, game-like style, and I am considering using a combination of instruments or a marimba.

The testing also revealed several practical issues affecting usability. Some users reported malfunctions with the play button, misalignment of the pause icon, abnormal drag behavior, overlapping fish positions, and unexpected interruptions during playback. These issues are critical because they impact the reliability of interactions. This feedback will be invaluable for my upcoming debugging efforts, which include: improving playback stability, optimizing hit detection and fish positioning logic, and preventing overlap errors. Overall, the peer testing confirmed that the project’s core concept is both engaging and unique, but it also indicated that the project currently requires clearer onboarding guidance, more refined interaction feedback, and further optimization of the sound and control systems.
